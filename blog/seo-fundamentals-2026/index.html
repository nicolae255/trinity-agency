6 SEO Fundamentals Belgian Businesses Should Get Right in 2026

Found July 2026 5 min read

Most small businesses chase SEO tactics — a keyword tool here, a backlink service there — before the fundamentals are actually in place. That's backwards. Whether you're targeting shoppers in Brussels, B2B buyers in Wallonie, or local search in Antwerp and the rest of Flanders, the same fundamentals apply before anything else is worth spending money on. Here they are, in order.

1. Make sure Google can actually crawl and understand your site

Before anything else, check that search engines can find and read your pages. Broken internal links, missing sitemaps, slow load times, and pages accidentally blocked from indexing are far more common than people expect — and no amount of content will help if Google can't reach it. Run your site through Google Search Console's Pages report and look at what's marked "Excluded"; it's usually the fastest way to spot the problem.

If your site runs in multiple languages — French, Dutch, and English is the norm for most Belgian businesses — check that your hreflang tags are implemented correctly too. A broken hreflang setup is one of the most common technical SEO mistakes we see on Belgian sites, and it quietly tells Google to show the wrong language version to the wrong audience.

2. Match content to real search intent, not guesses

The keyword with the highest search volume isn't always the right one to target. Someone searching "how does SEO work" wants an explanation; someone searching "SEO agency near me" wants to hire someone. Writing the wrong type of content for a keyword is one of the most common reasons pages rank but don't convert.

This matters even more in a multilingual market: a French-speaking visitor searching "agence SEO Bruxelles" and a Dutch-speaking visitor searching "SEO bureau Antwerpen" may have the exact same intent, but they need genuinely translated, locally-written pages — not a single page run through a translator — to actually convert once they land.

3. Fix your Google Business Profile before your blog

For most small, local businesses, an optimized Google Business Profile — accurate categories, complete hours, real photos, and a steady flow of reviews — drives more qualified traffic than a blog will for the first six months. Local SEO is usually the fastest path to visible results, and it's especially true in Belgium's city-based search behavior: someone searching for a service "in Brussels" or "in Gent" is often ready to buy today, not researching for later.

Keep your profile updated in the languages your customers actually search in, respond to reviews — ideally in the language they were left in — and make sure your categories match how people actually describe what you do, not internal jargon.

4. Internal linking is free authority

Every new page you publish should link to, and be linked from, relevant existing pages. This is the single most under-used SEO lever for small sites — it costs nothing and directly helps both users and search engines understand what your most important pages are.

Use descriptive anchor text instead of "click here" — a link that says "our SEO services" tells Google far more than one that says "learn more" — and build toward a hub-and-spoke structure: one strong overview page for each core service, linked out to and from every piece of supporting content you write about it.

5. Track rankings and traffic monthly, not sentiment

"I think our SEO is working" isn't a metric. Track a small set of target keywords, organic traffic, and the conversions that traffic produces — Google Search Console and Google Analytics cover most of what a small business needs, and neither costs anything. If a change doesn't move one of those three numbers within 60–90 days, it wasn't the right change.

Resist the urge to check rankings daily; search rankings fluctuate constantly, and a monthly view is far more useful than a daily one for making real decisions.

6. Don't ignore local backlinks and citations

Backlinks from big international sites are nice, but for local and regional businesses, being listed accurately on Belgian directories, your local chamber of commerce, sector associations, and regional press carries more weight than people expect — both for rankings and for the kind of trust signal that makes someone actually pick up the phone.

Consistency matters here too: your business name, address, and phone number — your "NAP" — should match exactly across every listing. Small inconsistencies, like "Rue de la Loi" in one place and "Rue de la Loi 200" in another, are a common, quietly damaging local SEO mistake.

The takeaway

SEO isn't a single trick — it's the compounding effect of getting the fundamentals right and staying consistent, in every language your customers search in. Start with crawlability, intent-matched content, and your Business Profile before spending on anything more advanced.

Frequently asked questions

How long does SEO take to work in Belgium?

Most businesses start seeing meaningful movement in rankings and traffic within 3 to 6 months of fixing the fundamentals above, though competitive terms in Brussels can take longer. Local SEO — Google Business Profile especially — tends to move faster, sometimes within a few weeks.

Do I need separate SEO for French and Dutch content?

Yes. Search behavior, keyword phrasing, and even search volume differ meaningfully between French and Dutch queries in Belgium — a direct translation of your French page is rarely enough to rank well for the equivalent Dutch search. Each language version needs its own keyword research and, ideally, its own genuinely written, not translated, content.

What's the difference between local SEO and regular SEO?

Local SEO focuses specifically on ranking for searches with local intent — "near me," a city name, or an implied local intent like "plumber" from someone in Brussels. It leans heavily on your Google Business Profile, local citations, and reviews, whereas general SEO covers your whole site's visibility for any relevant search, local or not. Most small businesses should prioritize local SEO first, since it converts faster.
